# Techniques for Clear Writing

Techniques for prose that delivers meaning to the reader with the least possible friction. They apply across every kind of writing.

---

## Words

1. **Pick the plain word** — "Use" beats "utilize." "Help" beats "facilitate." "Now" beats "at this juncture."
2. **Use concrete language** — "Improved performance" → "cut processing time by 40%."
3. **Avoid jargon, or define it on first use** — Don't assume the reader knows the term. If it has to appear, gloss it the first time.
4. **Cut empty intensifiers** — "Very," "really," "extremely," "incredibly." They puff up the line without adding anything.
5. **Cut filler phrases** — "Basically," "essentially," "the thing is," "at the end of the day." If removing the phrase doesn't change the meaning, remove it.
6. **Turn nominalizations back into verbs** — "Conduct a review" → "review." "Make a decision" → "decide." "Provide a recommendation" → "recommend."
7. **Avoid double negatives** — "Not impossible" → "possible." "Not uncommon" → "common." Make the reader invert once at most.
8. **Replace floating pronouns** — "This," "that," and "it" without a clear antecedent slow the reader down. Swap in the actual noun.
9. **One concept, one word** — Pick a term and stick with it through the piece. Don't switch between "user," "customer," and "client" if you mean the same person.
10. **Quantify** — "Many" → "83% of." "Significantly" → "three times." Numbers commit; vague modifiers don't.

---

## Sentences

11. **One idea per sentence** — If a sentence is doing two jobs, split it.
12. **Default to short sentences** — Aim for around 15–20 words. Long sentences are fine as the exception, not the rule.
13. **Keep subject and verb close** — Don't stack qualifying clauses between them; the reader loses track of who is doing what.
14. **Write in the active voice** — "The team shipped the report" beats "the report was shipped by the team."
15. **Use the passive on purpose, not by default** — Passive is the right choice when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or deliberately downplayed. Otherwise, active.
16. **Front-load the important information** — Conclusion or subject first; supporting material after.
17. **Keep modifiers close to what they modify** — A modifier far from its target invites misreading.
18. **Make parallel things grammatically parallel** — "Plan, design, and ship" — same form for each item in a list.
19. **Don't pile on connectives** — More than two or three "and / but / so" in a single sentence is a sign to split it.
20. **Prefer positive over negative phrasing** — "Arrive on time" beats "don't be late." Easier to parse, easier to act on.
21. **Conditions before conclusions** — "If A, then B" reads more cleanly than "B, in the case of A."

---

## Paragraphs and blocks

22. **One topic per paragraph.**
23. **Lead with the topic sentence** — The first sentence of the paragraph should preview what the paragraph is about.
24. **Keep paragraphs short** — Two to four sentences is a good default. Long paragraphs feel like work before the reader has read a word.
25. **Connect paragraphs logically** — Each paragraph should raise a question that the next paragraph answers.
26. **Use transitions to mark the relationship** — "However," "as a result," "by contrast," "specifically." They tell the reader what kind of move you're making.
27. **Use bullets for parallel items** — Three or more parallel things almost always read better as a list than as prose.
28. **Use numbered lists for order or priority** — When the sequence matters, number it.

---

## Document-level structure

29. **Lead with the conclusion (PREP)** — Point, Reason, Example, Point. State the takeaway, justify it, illustrate it, restate it.
30. **Inverted pyramid** — Most important information at the top; details below. Built for readers who stop early.
31. **Make the structure visible through headings** — A reader should be able to skim the headings alone and understand the shape of the piece.
32. **Headings should preview specific content** — "How monthly revenue grew 20% YoY" beats "Overview."
33. **Add a table of contents for long documents** — Anything substantial enough that a reader would want to jump should have one.
34. **Match heading granularity** — Sibling headings should be at the same level of abstraction. Don't put a chapter title and a footnote at the same indent.
35. **Known to unknown** — Start with what the reader already understands and use it as a bridge to the new information.
36. **General to specific** — Establish the big picture, then go into detail.
37. **Chronological for processes** — Sequences are most natural in the order they happen.
38. **By importance when readers might bounce** — If you can't count on them reaching the end, put the most important point first.
39. **Use contrast structures** — Before/after, problem/solution, old/new. The contrast itself does communication work.

---

## Visual and formatting

40. **Use whitespace** — Dense pages feel exhausting before the reader has read a word.
41. **Use bold for the scan layer** — A reader skimming should be able to land on the bolded words and still get the gist.
42. **Use tables for comparisons** — Anything with a comparison or correspondence structure is almost always clearer in a table.
43. **Use figures and screenshots** — When a picture or screenshot is faster than a description, use it.
44. **Mind type size and line height** — Physical readability is part of clarity. Don't ship body copy at 11px in a 1.1 line-height.
45. **Cap line length** — Lines that run too wide cause the eye to lose its place. For long-form web reading, around 60–80 characters per line.
46. **Use indentation to show hierarchy** — Nested structures should look nested.

---

## Reader awareness

47. **Match the reader's prior knowledge** — Vocabulary and explanation density should differ for an expert audience versus a beginner one.
48. **Anticipate the reader's questions** — When you can hear the reader thinking "but what about…?", answer it before they have to ask.
49. **Bridge with analogies** — Connect a new concept to a familiar one to make it land faster.
50. **Show specific examples** — After an abstract claim, give a concrete instance.
51. **Show counter-examples** — Saying what something *isn't* sharpens the boundary of what it *is*.
52. **Address likely objections** — Surface and answer the reader's "but…" before they reach the call to action.
53. **Make the next action explicit** — When the reader finishes, they should know exactly what to do.

---

## Editing and revision

54. **Read it out loud** — Awkward phrasing reveals itself the moment you hear it.
55. **Sleep on it** — Time creates distance. A piece that read fine last night reads differently in the morning.
56. **Cut anything you can cut without losing meaning** — Shorter is better when shorter still says the same thing.
57. **Run the "so what?" test** — For every claim, ask "so what does the reader do with this?" If the answer is unclear, cut or rework it.
58. **One pass per concern** — Edit for clarity in one pass, tone in another, specificity in a third. Trying to fix all three at once does each one badly.
59. **Have someone else read it** — Outside eyes catch the things your own eyes can't.
60. **Read it backwards, paragraph by paragraph** — Reading the last paragraph first, then the second-to-last, and so on, exposes paragraphs that don't stand on their own.
61. **Try to summarize it in one sentence** — If you can't, the focus is fuzzy.

---

## Logic and persuasion

62. **Claim, evidence, example** — A claim alone doesn't persuade. Always pair it with the reason and a concrete instance.
63. **Spell out cause and effect** — "Because A, B." "A leads to B." Don't make the reader infer the connection.
64. **Don't confuse correlation with causation** — A and B happening together isn't the same as A causing B.
65. **Combine quantitative and qualitative** — Numbers without story feel cold; story without numbers feels squishy. Use both.
66. **Cite sources** — The reader can verify, and your credibility goes up.
67. **State the conditions** — "Under X conditions" beats "in all cases." Honest scoping increases trust.
