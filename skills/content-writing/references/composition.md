# Composition

Composition is the most important part of writing. Lock the structure before you draft a sentence — every later decision rests on it.

Composition is *not* the prose itself. It is the arrangement of:

1. **Section design** — Which sections you'll have and the order they appear in.
2. **Section content** — What each heading, body, and close is doing.

You build composition on a notepad, not in finished prose. It is the architectural drawing that comes before the build. The actual writing follows the principles in SKILL.md.

**Terms used in this document:**
- **Piece** — One complete artifact. A single landing page, one email, one proposal, one article.
- **Section** — A block or division within the piece.
- **Composition** — How sections are designed and ordered.

There are two kinds of writing:

1. **Persuasive** — Writing that exists to change the reader's behavior or beliefs. Landing pages, ads, emails, sales decks.
2. **Informational** — Writing that exists to deliver knowledge or shared understanding. Reports, internal docs, manuals, briefs.

These two modes apply at the **section level**, not just the piece level. A persuasive landing page can contain an informational FAQ section. An informational report can contain a persuasive "why now" section. Knowing which mode each section is in is at the heart of good composition.

---

## The composition workflow

### Step 1: Define the reader's end state

Before you arrange anything, define **the state the reader should be in when they finish**. Every later judgment — what to include, what to cut, what order — uses this as the reference. If the end state is fuzzy, no decision about the structure can be definite.

### Step 2: Decide what to include and what to leave out

Use the end state to decide **what the piece must say and what it must not waste space on**. This is editorial selection at the structural level.

- In **persuasive** writing, distill the message to **one core point**. Every section exists to support that one point.
- In **informational** writing, include what the reader needs and nothing more. Even when several things are worth saying, cut whatever doesn't serve the goal.

### Step 3: Choose the sections and their order

Now decide **which sections appear and in what order**, given the end state.

**Tests for the lineup:**
- One section, one message. Don't compress.
- Each section should raise a question the next section answers.
- Anything missing? Anything redundant?

**The connective tissue:** Sections in a row aren't yet a composition. Each section should plant a question in the reader's head that the next section resolves. When that connection breaks, the reader feels "wait, why are we suddenly here?" and bounces.

| What the section just did | The question it leaves the reader with |
|---|---|
| Made a claim | "Why should I believe that?" → evidence |
| Named a problem | "How do I fix it?" → solution |
| Sketched the overview | "Show me the specifics" → details |
| Stated a fact | "What does that change?" → implications |
| Outlined a method | "Has this actually worked?" → proof |
| Summed up the whole thing | "So what should I do now?" → next step |

### Step 4: Sketch each section's direction

For each section in the lineup, decide its direction along three components.

**Heading** — The "promise" the section makes. The reader uses it to decide whether the rest of the section is worth their time.
- **Persuasive sections** — The heading is itself a hook. It needs to stop the reader and pull them down.
- **Informational sections** — The heading should describe the content cleanly and accurately. Ideally a reader can scan only the headings and reconstruct the structure of the piece.

**Body** — The body's job is to back up what the heading promised, to whatever level the reader needs in order to be convinced. Pick the form that fits:
- **Stated facts** — When objective fact is enough.
- **Specifics — numbers, examples, detail** — When the heading's claim is abstract and the reader can't picture it yet.
- **Evidence — data, results, third-party voice** — When credibility is in question.
- **Comparison / contrast** — When you want the reader to choose this option over alternatives.
- **Step-by-step process** — When you want the reader to think "I could actually do this."
- **Story** — When you want emotional pull, or when an experience communicates more than numbers.

**Close (optional)** — After the body has filled in the picture, restate the section's point one more time so it locks. Use this when the body is long. Skip it when the section is short.

## Composition by piece type

These are reference templates, not laws. Adjust sections to fit the actual brief. Use the four-step workflow to make the call; treat the templates as starting points.

### Landing page

**Structure:**

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Hero | In three seconds: "this is for someone like me." Headline + supporting line + visual + CTA + trust signals. |
| Problem / empathy | Put the reader's pain into words. They should feel understood. |
| Solution | The product/service framed as the resolution. Lead with benefit, not feature. |
| Why us | What separates this from the alternatives. Back it with numbers. |
| Results / proof | Concrete outcomes. Industry + problem + result, three points per case. |
| Testimonials / social proof | Real names, real photos, recognizable logos, press mentions, awards. |
| Product / service detail | What the reader is actually paying for: the features, mechanics, what's included. They should leave knowing exactly what they get. |
| Pricing | Plan comparison, price, what's included. Skip if the page deliberately doesn't disclose pricing. |
| How it works | A 3–5 step flow that makes adoption look manageable. |
| FAQ | The five to eight biggest unresolved doubts, addressed directly. |
| Guarantee / risk reversal | Refund policy, free trial, security posture. |
| Limited offer (optional) | "Free [bonus] this month," "first 50 only." Urgency or scarcity placed near the final CTA, only when it's actually true. |
| Final CTA + form | Inline, minimal fields, supporting microcopy that defuses last-mile hesitation. |

**LP-specific principles:**
- One page, one goal. Don't create competing paths.
- Strip the navigation. Don't give the reader exits.
- The hero must match the message of whatever brought the reader there (the ad, the email, the search).
- At least three CTAs: hero, mid-page, and at the close.

### Sales deck / proposal

**Structure:**

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Executive summary | One page that contains the whole story and the recommendation. Decision-makers may read only this. |
| Problem framing | Articulate the prospect's current situation back to them, accurately. Demonstrates that you understand. |
| Proposal | What you're recommending, with a clear line back to the problem. |
| Plan | Who does what, when. Phases and milestones. |
| Expected outcomes | Quantitative projections, with the reasoning under them. |
| Cost / terms | Pricing presented in a form that makes the ROI legible. |
| Track record / credibility | Comparable engagements, team profiles. |
| Next step | The specific action the reader should take to move forward. |

**Proposal-specific principles:**
- Open with the prospect's problem, not your company.
- Give them a "why now." Inertia is the default; you're competing with it.
- Use numbers. "We will improve performance" is too soft to land.
- Use *their* language. Their internal terms, their industry shorthand. Not your jargon.
- Assume the reader skims. The headings and visuals should carry the argument on their own.
- Surface the risks and reservations yourself. Hiding them costs trust.

### Email (sales / marketing)

**Structure:**

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Subject line | Earn the open. Pull from attention-hooks. |
| First line | Visible in the preview pane. Establish the reason to read in one sentence. |
| Body | One message. Short. |
| CTA | One email, one action. One button or one link. |
| P.S. (optional) | One of the most-read parts of an email. Use it to repeat the key point or the CTA. |

**Email-specific principles:**
- Short wins. The goal is no scroll.
- One email = one message = one CTA.
- Whatever the subject line promised, the body must deliver. A bait-and-switch costs you future opens.

### Blog post / article

**Structure:**

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Title | Match the reader's search intent and earn the click. |
| Lead | Who this is for and what they'll get. Establish the reason to keep reading. |
| Table of contents (long pieces) | Show the shape of the piece and let the reader jump. |
| Body sections | One topic per heading. Built to be skimmable. |
| Close | Recap of the key points + the next action (related reading, a CTA). |

**Article-specific principles:**
- The reader should be able to skim only the headings and get the argument.
- Avoid long paragraphs. Two to four sentences is the sweet spot.
- Use lists, tables, and pull quotes to vary the visual rhythm.
- Resolve the search intent in the opening before going deep.

### Press release

**Structure:**

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Headline | The core news in one line, stated as fact. No hype. |
| Subhead (optional) | Background detail or a key number that supports the headline. |
| Lead paragraph | The 5W1H — who, what, when, where, why, how — compressed into one paragraph. The story should be clear from this paragraph alone. |
| Body | Detail behind the lead: background, specifics, quoted statements from the people involved. |
| Boilerplate | Standard company description: name, founded, what the company does, URL. |
| Contact | Press contact: name, email, phone. |

**Press-release-specific principles:**
- Strict inverted pyramid. The most important information first; later paragraphs should be droppable without breaking the story.
- Stick to facts. No adjectives or subjective evaluation. Skip "groundbreaking," "revolutionary," "industry-leading."
- Put opinion in quoted statements from named people.
- Write at a level of detail a journalist could publish as-is.

### Case study

**Structure:**

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Title | Lead with the outcome. "How [Company] [achieved result]." Or invert from the outcome to grab attention: "200% revenue growth — and the problem they had to solve to get there." |
| Customer profile | Industry, size, context. Lets the reader recognize "this is someone like us." |
| Challenge | What they were stuck on before. Specific pain, specific numbers. |
| Approach | What they did. Why this approach, the rollout process. |
| Results | Quantitative outcomes (X% improvement, Y dollars saved). Before/after comparison. |
| Customer voice (optional) | Named quote from the actual person. Strongest when it carries both fact and feeling. |

**Case-study-specific principles:**
- Don't break the challenge → approach → result chain.
- Outcomes must be quantified. "We're happy with the results" is not enough.
- Include enough specific detail that the reader recognizes their own situation in it.

### Manual / help documentation

**Structure:**

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Title | What the reader will be able to do. "Configuring X." "How to do Y." |
| Prerequisites (optional) | Required environment, prep, target reader's level. |
| Steps | Numbered actions, one action per step. Use screenshots and diagrams. |
| Verification | What the screen should look like when the steps are done correctly. |
| Troubleshooting (optional) | Common errors and how to fix them. |
| Related docs (optional) | Links to the next thing the reader probably needs. |

**Manual-specific principles:**
- One page, one task. Don't bundle unrelated tasks.
- The numbered steps should contain only what the reader does. Background and rationale go in side text or callouts.
- Don't assume prior knowledge. Define terms on first use.
- Lean on screenshots and diagrams. Pure-text instructions are easy to lose your place in.
