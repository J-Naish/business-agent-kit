# Attention Hooks

The opening line is doing one job: keep the reader from leaving. Headlines, leads, ad copy, social posts, subject lines, video openers — anywhere the next click depends on the first few words.

Patterns combine. The strongest hooks usually layer two or three (counter-intuition + a concrete number, audience call-out + direct address + story). A single pattern reads like a tactic; layered patterns read like the writer knew exactly who they were talking to.

---

## Fact-check rules (non-negotiable)

The whole point of a hook is the strong claim. That's also why a single fabricated detail destroys the rest of the piece — the reader's trust collapses the second they sense it.

- **Don't invent numbers or results.** "10,000 customers," "500 companies," "30% increase" — only use specifics that came from the user or that you've verified. A plausible-sounding number is still a fake number.
- **Don't invent credentials.** "Former Google engineer," "award-winning designer," "expert in X" — only when there's a real basis.
- **Don't fake scarcity.** "Limited to 50," "ends Friday," "private beta" — only if the constraint is actually real. Don't manufacture deadlines or supply caps.
- **Don't exaggerate losses or risk.** "Costing you $500/month," "your business won't survive" — only with a defensible reason behind it.
- **When you can't substantiate, switch to structural hooks.** Counter-intuition, questions, story, audience call-out — these work without depending on a verifiable claim.
- **When the fact is missing, leave a labeled placeholder, not a fudge.** `[X% improvement]` is honest; "significant improvement" pretending to be a specific number is not. `[Customer name]`, `[Specific result]`, `[Date]` — drop them in and ask for the real value before shipping.

## When not to use hooks

Hooks are an attention weapon. Aimed at the wrong audience, they backfire. Dial them down or skip entirely in:

- **Formal B2B documents** — Proposals, quotes, contracts. Trust and accuracy come first; punchy copy reads as unserious.
- **Internal documents** — Status updates, reports, internal wikis. The reader is already obligated; hooks read as performative.
- **Press releases** — Accuracy is the product. Power words and counter-intuition undermine credibility.
- **Regulated topics** — Health, finance, legal. Overstatement creates real legal exposure.
- **Trust-recovery moments** — Apologies, incident reports, complaint responses. A punchy opener fans the fire.
- **Reference content** — Spec sheets, FAQs, manuals. The reader came for an answer; clarity beats hooks.

## 1. Counter-intuitive

Break the reader's default assumption in a single line. The contradiction stops the scroll because the brain refuses to leave it unresolved.

**Tactics:**
- **Reject the conventional wisdom.** State the opposite of what most people in the field believe.
- **Defy the expected outcome.** Lead with a result that shouldn't logically have happened, given the setup.
- **Reveal hidden mechanics.** Show the part of the machine the reader didn't know was there.
- **Use an order-of-magnitude number.** A figure so far outside the expected range that the reader thinks "that can't be right."
- **Set two things side by side.** The bigger the gap between them, the harder the pull.
- **Borrow a metaphor from a different domain.** Sometimes one good comparison does what a paragraph of explanation can't.

**Examples:**
- "We doubled the price. Conversions went up."
- "I shut off every paid ad we were running. Revenue grew."
- "The hardest-working person on your team is the bottleneck."
- "Sent 500 cold emails. Got an 87% reply rate. The trick wasn't the copy."
- "Most of what you've been told about cold outreach is a strategy from 2015 that's still being sold in 2025."
- "SEO is farming. Paid is hunting. If you're confused why one of them isn't working, you're probably doing the other one."
- "The $0-budget founder beat the venture-funded incumbent. The reason has nothing to do with the product."

**Caution:** A counter-intuitive claim with nothing under it is just clickbait. The body has to deliver. If numbers are involved, the fact-check rules above are absolute.

## 2. Concrete and useful

Speak directly to a problem the reader has *right now*. Pull them in with executable specificity instead of abstract benefit.

**Tactics:**
- **Name the exact problem in their own words.** Don't paraphrase the pain. State it the way they'd describe it to a colleague.
- **Replace vague modifiers with numbers.** "Faster" is forgettable. "Three minutes instead of forty" is not.
- **Show the payoff is fast.** "Today," "in five minutes," "by the end of this email."
- **Count the steps.** "Three steps," "in this order." Reproducibility is its own pull.
- **Reframe the number into the unit that hurts.** Same fact, different scale, different impact.

**Examples:**
- "Close the books in 15 minutes. The exact spreadsheet, free, no email required."
- "The five interview questions you'll definitely get. And the answer that made the difference for the last three people I coached."
- "Stop writing copy for a fictional 'target customer.' Here's the 90-second exercise that replaces it."
- "$365 a year" → "A dollar a day to know you're not the cause."
- "30% success rate" → "One in three came in with zero experience."

## 3. Psychological triggers

The well-documented levers — authority, social proof, scarcity, loss aversion, urgency, curiosity. Some present external evidence; others act directly on the reader's emotional system.

**Tactics:**
- **Ride a current trend.** Anchor the piece to something already in the conversation. Hard to ignore what everyone is already discussing.
- **Show the social proof.** "Used by 50,000 teams," "trusted by [recognizable logos]." Triggers the fear of being the only one who didn't get the memo.
- **Lean on authority.** Specific credentials, specific publications, specific results. "An ex-Stripe engineer on…" is a click; "experts say" is not.
- **Frame the bandwagon.** "Spreading rapidly through SaaS," "every fund I talk to is now using…" The fear of being late is sharper than the desire to be early.
- **Use real scarcity.** "Closed the cohort at 100," "private until now," "first time outside the team." Hard-to-get reads as valuable.
- **Frame the loss, not the gain.** Loss aversion outperforms equivalent gain framing in almost every test ever run.
- **Put a clock on it.** "Today only," "before Friday," "by end of quarter." A deadline forces a decision now.
- **Voice the reader's frustration verbatim.** "You get me" creates trust faster than any credential.
- **Open the curiosity gap.** Show part of the answer; withhold the rest. The brain hates an unfinished sentence.
- **Frame the list around what to *avoid*.** "Five mistakes that…" outperforms "five tips that…" almost every time.

**Examples:**
- "An ex-Google search engineer on what actually moves rankings in 2026 (and what every SEO blog still gets wrong)."
- "500 SaaS teams switched off [Tool] in the last twelve months. The pattern in why is the interesting part."
- "Internal pricing playbook from a $40M ARR company. The first time it's been outside the building."
- "Leaving this one default setting on costs the average ad account $24,000 a year."
- "You start strong on Monday and you're done by Wednesday. The reason isn't discipline."
- "We tripled revenue last year. The thing we refused to change is the thing every consultant told us to."
- "Seven things I won't let any landing page I work on do."
- "The three behaviors I see in every interview that doesn't get the offer."

## 4. Power words

The raw force of the language itself. Place words that fire emotion, vivid imagery, or social charge — and place them deliberately, not constantly.

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Taboo | Death, money, sex, drugs, addiction, lies — words culture tells you not to say in polite company draw the eye. |
| Forbidden / warning | Stop, never, fatal, irreversible, last chance, before it's too late, killed, banned |
| Reveal / secret | Exposed, leaked, off the record, kept quiet, the part nobody tells you, what they don't want you to see |
| Negation / collapse | It was all a lie, falling apart, broken, dead, theater, scam |
| Extreme / superlative | Unprecedented, insane, off the charts, beyond belief, in a different league, ruthless |
| Loss / fear | Wiped out, fatal mistake, bankrupt, gone for good, no coming back, eats your margin alive |
| Transformation | A different person, night and day, no going back, finally clicked, woke up, snapped |
| Provocation | Still doing X? Seriously? You sure about that? Soft. Lazy. That's it? |

**Examples:**
- "Stop running ads until you read this."
- "I'm going to say what nobody in the recruiting industry will say out loud."
- "There's a $30,000 hole in your tax return. You've been walking past it for four years."
- "Killed every product line that was profitable. Kept the one losing money. The reason is still working three years later."
- "Half the founders I came up with in YC were on Adderall. None of them say it on the podcasts."
- "100 women in two years. He's average-looking, works in accounting, and sends the same two-line opener to every match."

**Cautions:**
- Stack too many power words and the line tips into clickbait. One or two per message, only when the body delivers.
- **Taboo words are the highest-yield and the highest-risk lever.** Sex, death, profanity, anything socially loaded — only when the brand, channel, and audience can absorb it. The test isn't "does this grab attention." The test is "does the reader still trust me five seconds later."

## 5. Storytelling

Story bypasses analytical reading. Narrative gets through the door that "facts about a product" gets stopped at.

**Tactics:**
- **Sharp before/after.** Open in the wreckage. The bigger the gap from there to here, the stronger the pull.
- **Failure first, comeback second.** The version where the protagonist fails before they win is the version people read.
- **Tell it through someone else.** A customer, a colleague, a peer. Third-person reads as more credible than the company telling its own story.
- **Open in medias res.** Skip the setup. Start at the most charged moment and loop back. Quoting someone's exact words at the start ("'You have ninety days,' the doctor said.") sharpens it further.
- **Hypothetical scenario.** "Imagine if…" engages the reader's imagination involuntarily. Useful when the actual event hasn't happened.
- **Future projection.** Show the reader's life six weeks from now in specifics they can feel.

**Examples:**
- "From $30K in credit-card debt to a $1.2M business in 24 months. The first ten months was the part nobody likes to talk about."
- "I was on the phone with the bank when I noticed something on the statement. By morning, I'd rewritten the entire offer."
- "Three months in, churn dropped from 11% to 4.3%. The Acme customer success lead on the meeting where it shifted."
- "The phone rang. The bank's number was on the screen. I checked the balance and my legs went out from under me."
- "'You're going to lose the company.' That sentence rearranged the next three years."
- "If Google's algorithm changed completely tomorrow, would your site still be standing? Mine wouldn't have, eight months ago."
- "Six weeks from now, you'll wake up before the alarm. Opening the laptop will be the part of the day you're looking forward to. I know how that sounds. I'm telling you what changed for me."

## 6. Audience call-out

Name the reader so specifically that scrolling past it feels like ignoring something addressed by name. The narrower the call-out, the harder the pull.

**Tactics:**
- **Name the role and the situation.** Title, years in, specific scenario.
- **Describe the room they're in right now.** Make the line read like you're sitting next to them.
- **Name the stage of the journey.** "Just starting," "stuck at the next plateau," "ready for the next move."

**Examples:**
- "For ecommerce founders stuck under $100K MRR for a third quarter in a row."
- "32. Three job changes. No specialist credential. You can still get a 40% raise this year. I did, and I'll show you the conversation that did it."
- "You installed Google Analytics. You have no idea what you're supposed to look at. This is for you."
- "Your team just crossed five direct reports and your management style stopped working overnight. Read this before your one-on-ones tomorrow."

**Caution:** A narrower call-out trades reach for impact. Pick the trade deliberately.

---

## 7. Direct address

Collapse the distance. Make a piece written for thousands feel like one person speaking to one person. Weak alone, strong as the entry to another pattern.

**Tactics:**
- **Ask a real question.** A direct question forces the reader's brain to start composing an answer, which is hard to do while scrolling away.
- **Open with a confession.** Lead with a real failure or weakness of your own. The candor itself earns trust.
- **Drop into the conspiratorial register.** "Just between us." "I'd never say this on a sales call." Manufactures intimacy in one line.

**Examples:**
- "When was the last time you actually opened your own site on your phone? Not the staging build. The real one."
- "Honest admission: this product has one real flaw. I'm going to tell you what it is before I tell you anything else."
- "I'd never say this in front of a client. I'm going to say it now."
- "I spent three years doing this completely wrong. The thing that finally made me see it took thirty seconds."

## Combining patterns

Layered hooks are almost always stronger than single-pattern hooks. The combinations below aren't formulas — they're examples of moves that work in pairs.

| Combination | Example |
|---|---|
| Counter-intuitive + concrete number | "Tripled ad spend last quarter. Profit got cut in half. The thing that broke wasn't the ads." |
| Authority + loss aversion | "An ex-IRS auditor: that expense category is going to get rejected, and the penalty starts the day you file." |
| Story + power words | "$300K of ad spend went up in smoke that day. The one thing it taught me is still saving the agency money four years later." |
| Social proof + curiosity gap | "3,000 companies installed it. Only the ones that hit their goal had this one initial setting in common." |
| Concrete + loss aversion | "Five default settings in Google Ads. Leave them alone and you'll bleed $20K a year, and the dashboards won't tell you about any of it." |
| Audience call-out + direct address | "Stuck at 1,000 followers? I'll be honest with you. It isn't the content." |
| Audience call-out + counter-intuitive | "The people grinding hardest on SEO are the ones whose rankings are quietly dropping." |
| Direct address + story | "Hear me out. Last month I shut off every dollar of paid acquisition. Week three was the part I wasn't expecting." |
| Counter-intuitive + story | "We killed every product that was actually selling. Kept the one nobody liked. Three years on, it's the only one still here." |
| Power words + audience call-out + concrete | "Still running ads on gut feel? Soft. Look at these three numbers and tell me you're sure." |
| Concrete number + story + curiosity gap | "An $8K/month clinic became a three-month waitlist in six months. The change had nothing to do with the treatment, the pricing, or the marketing." |
