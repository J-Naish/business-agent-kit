# Attention Hooks

Patterns for grabbing the reader and pulling them into the next sentence. Use them in headlines, leads, ad copy, social posts, email subject lines, video openers — anywhere you have a few seconds to earn the rest of the piece.

Patterns combine. The strongest hooks usually layer two or three (e.g. counter-intuitive + concrete number, or audience call-out + direct address + story).

---

## Fact-check rules (non-negotiable)

Hooks earn attention with strong claims. That's exactly why a single fabricated detail destroys trust instantly. Hold the line on the following:

- **Don't invent numbers or results.** "10,000 customers," "500 companies," "30% improvement" — only use specifics that came from the user or that you've verified through research. Plausible-sounding numbers are still made up.
- **Don't invent credentials.** "Former engineer at X," "award-winning Y," "expert in Z" — only when there's a real basis.
- **Don't fake scarcity.** "Limited to 50 spots," "ends Friday," "private preview" — only if the constraint is actually real. Don't manufacture deadlines or supply caps.
- **Don't exaggerate losses or risk.** "Costing you $500/month," "your business won't survive" — only if there's a defensible reason.
- **When you can't substantiate, lean on structural hooks.** Counter-intuition, questions, story, audience call-out — these work without depending on a verifiable claim.

## When not to use hooks

Hooks are an attention-grabbing tool. In some contexts they backfire. Dial them down or skip them entirely in:

- **Formal B2B documents** — Proposals, quotes, contracts. Trust and accuracy come first; punchy copy reads as unserious.
- **Internal documents** — Status updates, reports, internal wikis. The reader is already obligated to read; hooks read as performance.
- **Press releases** — Accuracy is the product. Power words and counter-intuition undermine credibility.
- **Regulated topics** — Health, finance, legal. Overstatement creates real legal exposure.
- **Trust-recovery moments** — Apologies, incident reports, complaint responses. Punchy openers fan the fire.
- **Reference content** — Spec sheets, FAQs, manuals. The reader already wants the answer; clarity beats hooks.

## 1. Counter-intuitive

Subvert the reader's default assumption. The contradiction stops the scroll.

**Tactics:**
- **Reject the conventional wisdom** — State the opposite of what most people believe.
- **Defy the expected outcome** — Lead with a result the reader's mental model says shouldn't happen.
- **Hidden mechanics** — Reveal a fact, dataset, or causal chain that isn't widely known.
- **Order-of-magnitude numbers** — Use a number so far outside the expected range that the reader thinks "wait, really?"
- **Contrast** — Set two things side by side; the gap itself is the hook. The bigger the gap, the harder the pull.
- **Metaphor / analogy** — Borrow a frame from a different domain to make a point land in one phrase.

**Examples:**
- "We cut ad spend in half. Sales tripled."
- "Protein shakes won't build muscle."
- "Waking up at 5 a.m. is hurting your output."
- "Sent 500 cold emails. Got an 87% reply rate."
- "The $0-budget founder beat the venture-funded incumbent. Here's how."
- "SEO is farming. Paid ads is hunting. Which one does your business actually need?"

**Caution:** A counter-intuitive claim with nothing under it just looks like clickbait. The body has to back it up. If numbers are involved, hold the fact-check rules.

## 2. Concrete and useful

Speak directly to a problem the reader has *right now*. Pull them in with executable specificity.

**Tactics:**
- **Name the problem** — Restate the reader's actual pain in their own words.
- **Concrete numbers** — Replace vague modifiers with figures.
- **Immediate payoff** — "Today," "in five minutes," "by the end of this email." Show the result is fast.
- **Step counts** — "Three steps," "in this order." Reproducibility is its own hook.
- **Reframing the number** — The same fact lands differently in different units. Convert into the scale that hits the reader hardest.

**Examples:**
- "Close out monthly expenses in 15 minutes. The template inside."
- "From 0 to 1,000 followers in 30 days: the seven things that actually moved it."
- "The five interview questions you'll definitely get — and how to double your hit rate."
- "$365/year for insurance" → "Less than a dollar a day to cover your whole family."
- "30% success rate" → "1 in 3 career-changers landed the role with no prior experience."

## 3. Psychological triggers

Borrow well-documented levers — authority, social proof, scarcity, loss aversion, curiosity. Some present external evidence; others act directly on the reader's emotions.

**Tactics:**
- **Riding a trend** — Anchor the piece to something currently in the news, the cultural conversation, a meme. Hard to ignore what everyone is already discussing.
- **Social proof** — "Used by 50,000 teams," "trusted by [logos]." Triggers the fear of being the one left out.
- **Authority** — Credentials, media mentions, awards. A trust shortcut: if *they* say it, it must be worth a look.
- **Bandwagon** — "Spreading rapidly through [industry]." The fear of falling behind.
- **Scarcity** — "Limited to 100," "private," "first time public." Hard-to-get reads as valuable.
- **Loss aversion (FOMO)** — Frame the cost of *not* acting. Loss hits harder than equivalent gain.
- **Urgency** — "Today only," "before Friday," "if you wait." A clock makes people decide now.
- **Empathy** — Voice the reader's frustration verbatim. "You get me" creates trust.
- **Curiosity gap** — Show part of the answer, withhold the rest. The brain hates an unfinished sentence.
- **Negative-list framing** — "Five mistakes that…" "What top performers never do." Loss-framed lists outperform gain-framed ones.

**Examples:**
- "How ChatGPT actually changed the writer's job."
- "The morning routine 100,000 people swear by."
- "An ex-Google engineer on what really moves search rankings."
- "500 companies switched this year. Here's what they all wanted to fix."
- "Internal playbook — public for the first time."
- "Leaving this setting on is costing the average team $2,400 a year."
- "You start strong every Monday and quit by Wednesday. Why does that keep happening?"
- "We tripled revenue in a year. The one thing we refused to change."
- "Seven things you should never do on a landing page."
- "Three behaviors that quietly tank every interview."

## 4. Power words

Lean on the raw force of the language itself. Strategically place words that fire strong emotion or vivid imagery.

**Categories:**

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Taboo | Death, money, sex, drugs, addiction, lies — words society tells you not to say in public draw the eye. |
| Forbidden / warning | Stop, never, fatal, irreversible, last chance, before it's too late |
| Reveal / secret | Exposed, leaked, off the record, kept quiet, the part nobody tells you |
| Negation / collapse | It was all a lie, falling apart, broken, dead, theater |
| Extreme / superlative | Unprecedented, insane, off the charts, beyond belief, in a different league |
| Loss / fear | Wiped out, fatal mistake, bankrupt, gone for good, no coming back |
| Transformation | A different person, night and day, no going back, finally clicked, woke up |
| Provocation | Still doing X? Seriously? You sure? Is that all? Soft. |

**Examples:**
- "Still grinding on SEO?"
- "Recruiting industry: I'm telling you what we don't say out loud."
- "There's a $30K-a-year hole in your tax return. You just don't see it."
- "Worked myself into the ground. Then I figured out the actual problem wasn't effort."

**Cautions:**
- Stack too many power words and the line tips into clickbait. One or two per message, only when the body delivers.
- **Taboo words are the most powerful and the highest-risk lever.** Sex, death, profanity, anything socially loaded — only use when the brand, channel, and audience can absorb it. The test isn't "does it grab attention," it's "does trust survive after I grab attention."

## 5. Storytelling

Story bypasses analytical reading. People react to narrative more strongly than to facts and remember it longer.

**Tactics:**
- **Dramatic before/after** — Sharp transformation from low to high. The bigger the gap, the stronger the pull.
- **Failure to comeback** — Open in the wreckage; the rest of the piece is how the protagonist climbed out.
- **Third-party account** — Tell someone else's story, not your own. Customer, employee, peer. Reads as more credible.
- **Open in medias res** — Start at the climax or the most charged moment, then loop back to how it got there. Quoting someone's actual words ("'You're going to lose the company,' he said.") sharpens the immediacy.
- **Hypothetical scenario** — "Imagine if…" engages the reader's imagination involuntarily. Useful when the actual event hasn't happened.
- **Future projection** — Describe the reader's own life three months from now. Specific enough to make them want to live it.

**Examples:**
- "From $30K in debt to a $1M business in two years."
- "I was about to shut off all our paid ads when I noticed something."
- "Three months in, churn dropped by half. The customer success lead at Acme on the one thing that changed."
- "The phone rang. The bank's number on the screen. I checked the balance and my legs went out from under me."
- "'If we keep going like this, we won't make it through the quarter.' That sentence rearranged the whole company."
- "If Google's algorithm changed completely tomorrow, would your site still be standing?"
- "Three months from now, you'll wake up before your alarm. Opening the laptop will be the part of the day you look forward to."

## 6. Audience call-out

Name the reader so specifically that scrolling past it feels like ignoring something addressed to them. The more specific the criteria, the harder the hook.

**Tactics:**
- **Direct attribute call-out** — Age, role, title, years of experience, industry.
- **Situation portrait** — Describe the exact scene the reader is in right now.
- **Stage of journey** — "Just getting started," "stuck at the next plateau," "ready for the next level."

**Examples:**
- "For ecommerce founders stuck under $100K in monthly revenue."
- "30s, three job changes, no specialist credentials. You can still raise your salary."
- "You installed Google Analytics. You have no idea what to look at. This is for you."
- "You crossed five direct reports and your management style stopped working overnight. Read this."

**Caution:** The narrower the call-out, the smaller the addressable audience. Trade off depth of impact against reach deliberately.

---

## 7. Direct address

Collapse the distance with the reader. Make a piece written for thousands feel like one person speaking to one person. Weak on its own, strong as the entry point to another pattern.

**Tactics:**
- **Question** — A direct question forces the reader's brain to start composing an answer. Hard to scroll past mid-thought.
- **Confession / vulnerability** — Open with a real failure or weakness. The candor itself earns trust.
- **Conspiratorial register** — "Just between us." "I'd never say this on a sales call." Manufactures intimacy.

**Examples:**
- "Have you actually opened your own site on your phone?"
- "Honest admission: this product has one real flaw. Here it is."
- "I'd never say this in front of a client, but —"
- "I spent three years doing it completely wrong. Here's what finally made me see it."

## Combining patterns

Layered hooks are almost always stronger than single-pattern hooks.

| Combination | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| Counter-intuitive + concrete number | "Tripled ad spend. Profit got cut in half." |
| Authority + loss aversion | "A former IRS auditor: that expense category is going to get rejected in your audit." |
| Story + power words | "$300K of ad spend went up in smoke that day. Here's the one thing it taught me." |
| Social proof + curiosity gap | "3,000 companies installed it. Only the ones that hit their goal had this one initial setting in common." |
| Concrete + loss aversion | "Five default settings in Google Ads. Leave them alone and you'll bleed $20K a year." |
| Audience call-out + direct address | "Stuck at 1,000 followers? I'll be honest with you — it isn't your content." |
| Audience call-out + counter-intuitive | "The people grinding hardest on SEO are the ones whose rankings are dropping." |
| Direct address + story | "Hear me out. Last month I shut off every dollar of paid acquisition." |
| Counter-intuitive + story | "We killed every product that was selling. Kept the one with the worst reviews." |
| Power words + audience call-out + concrete | "Still running ads on gut feel? Seriously? Look at these three numbers." |
| Concrete number + story + curiosity gap | "An $8K/month clinic became a three-month waitlist. The change had nothing to do with the treatment." |
