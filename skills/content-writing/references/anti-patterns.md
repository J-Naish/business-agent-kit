# Writing Anti-Patterns

Common ways prose goes wrong. Use this list as a self-check while drafting, as a reviewer's checklist for someone else's copy, and as a problem-finder during editing.

---

## Setup and planning

1. **Writing without a reader in mind** — Drafting before defining who the piece is for.
2. **Writing without a goal** — Drafting before deciding what the reader should do, feel, or believe afterward.
3. **Trying to land with everyone** — Refusing to narrow the audience, ending up generic enough to land with no one.
4. **Skimping on research** — Starting before you have the material, ending up with a thin, uninformed piece.
5. **No outline** — Writing in stream-of-consciousness order. The logic skips, doubles back, and never quite arrives.
6. **Multiple goals stuffed into one piece** — Trying to inform, persuade, and sell in the same breath. Each one ends up half-done.

---

## Selecting what to say

7. **Saying everything** — Failing to filter, leaving the reader unsure what they're supposed to take away.
8. **Writing what *you* want to say** — Leading with what's interesting to the writer instead of what's useful to the reader.
9. **Stacking facts** — Listing information without ever answering "so what?"
10. **Drowning in detail** — So much fine-grained explanation that the main argument disappears underneath it.
11. **No hierarchy** — Treating every point as equally important, leaving the reader with no sense of priority.
12. **Tolerating duplication** — Saying the same thing twice in different words, costing the reader time.

---

## Structure

13. **Conclusion buried at the end** — Readers don't always reach the end. If the takeaway only lives in the final paragraph, most readers will miss it.
14. **Long runway before takeoff** — Burning the opening on background, history, and self-introduction before getting to the point.
15. **Logical jumps** — Going from A to C without explaining the connection. The reader stops and asks "wait, why?"
16. **Disconnected sections** — Sections that read as standalone bricks. The reader keeps wondering "why are we suddenly on this?"
17. **Multiple ideas per section** — Cramming two or three arguments into a unit that should hold one.
18. **Headings that don't preview the content** — "Overview," "Details," "Other." Empty labels that tell the reader nothing.
19. **Mismatched granularity** — Mixing high-level and detail-level points at the same hierarchical level.
20. **Tapering off** — A sharp opening that loses energy and care as the piece goes on.
21. **Weak or missing call to action** — Not telling the reader what to do next, in a piece that's meant to drive action.

---

## Sentences

22. **Sentence too long** — Stringing clauses together until a single sentence is doing the work of three.
23. **Subject and verb too far apart** — So much qualifying material between them that the reader loses track of who is doing what.
24. **Ambiguous modifiers** — A modifier that could plausibly attach to two different things, leaving meaning unsettled.
25. **Passive voice without a reason** — Passive constructions that hide the actor and end up sounding evasive ("mistakes were made").
26. **Double negatives** — "Not impossible," "not unlikely." Forcing the reader to invert twice to get to the point.
27. **Monotonous rhythm** — Every sentence the same length, the same shape. The prose flattens out and the reader's attention drifts.

---

## Word choice

28. **Stacking abstractions** — "Optimize," "leverage," "innovative," "synergy." Vocabulary that sounds substantial and means nothing.
29. **Business-speak overload** — "Solution," "deliverable," "circle back," "bandwidth." Words your reader doesn't actually use.
30. **Empty intensifiers** — "Very," "really," "extremely," "incredibly." They add length without adding meaning.
31. **Nominalization** — "Conduct an investigation" instead of "investigate." "Make a decision" instead of "decide." Turning verbs into nouns to inflate sentences.
32. **Synonym stacking** — "Improve, enhance, and elevate." Listing near-synonyms to fill space.
33. **Filler phrases** — "Basically," "essentially," "the thing is," "at the end of the day." Cut them and nothing changes except length.
34. **Hyperbole without backing** — "Revolutionary," "unparalleled," "game-changing." Loud claims with nothing under them eat your credibility.

---

## Persuasion and credibility

35. **Claims without support** — "It works." "Customers love it." Why? How much? Says who?
36. **Vague proof** — "Many customers," "significantly improved." No numbers, no examples — nothing the reader can grip.
37. **Cherry-picking** — Showing only the wins, hiding the limitations. Readers can usually feel this and trust drops.
38. **Ignoring obvious objections** — Leaving "but what about…?" hanging. The reader carries that doubt all the way to the CTA and doesn't click.
39. **Fabricated social proof** — Invented testimonials, fake logos, made-up adoption numbers.
40. **Hollow appeals to authority** — "Experts agree," "studies show," with no specific expert or study named.
41. **All emotion, no logic** — Pushing on fear, urgency, or empathy without any reasoning the reader can verify.
42. **Spec dump** — Listing features without translating them into what the reader actually gets.

---

## Tone and posture

43. **Talking down to the reader** — Lecturing, condescension, "as you may not know." Especially toxic in B2C.
44. **Tone drift** — Casual at the top, formal in the middle, breezy again at the close. The voice doesn't feel like one person.
45. **Wrong distance from the reader** — Too familiar with strangers, too stiff with people you actually know. The register doesn't match the relationship.
46. **Self-congratulation** — "Our amazing service," "our innovative platform." When you say it about yourself, it reads as hollow.
47. **Pushy** — "Act now," "don't miss out," "you must." Stacked urgency raises suspicion instead of converting.
48. **Hedging everything** — "It might be the case that this could potentially…" Soft language piled on soft language until the reader can't tell what you actually believe.

---

## Reader experience

49. **Visually punishing** — Walls of text, no breaks, no whitespace. The reader bounces before reading a word.
50. **Paragraphs too long** — Ten-line blocks that feel like work just to look at.
51. **Prose where a list belongs** — Burying parallel items inside paragraphs so the reader can't compare them at a glance.
52. **Unexplained jargon** — Using terms the audience may not know, with no gloss on first use.
53. **Pronoun fog** — "This," "that," "it" piling up until no one knows what any of them refer to.
54. **Unscannable** — No headings, no bold, no lists. A reader skimming for the point can't find it.
55. **Bait-and-switch headlines** — A title that promises something the body doesn't deliver. Pays for one click and burns the next ten.

---

## Editing and revision

56. **Skipping the edit pass** — Shipping the first draft. Typos, logic gaps, and bloat all survive.
57. **Refusing to cut** — Keeping a sentence because you wrote it, not because it earns its place.
58. **Falling in love with your own phrasing** — A clever line that the reader has to work to parse is a clever line that should go.
59. **Editing for everything at once** — Trying to fix clarity, tone, and specificity in a single pass. Each gets done badly.
60. **Writing in a closed loop** — Never showing it to anyone else. Your blind spots stay blind.
