## 1. Principles Across All Formats

### 1-1. Creative Drives Performance

In modern Meta ads, creative quality and diversity are often more important than small targeting or bid adjustments. The creative tells Meta what the offer is, who it is for, and why users should care.

### 1-2. Copy Length

| Element | Recommended length | What happens if it is long |
|---|---:|---|
| **Primary text** | Up to 125 characters before truncation risk | May collapse behind "See more" |
| **Headline** | Up to 27 characters before truncation risk | May be truncated |
| **Description** | Usually 25-30 characters; Facebook Feed commonly uses ~27 | Often hidden depending on placement |

Note: descriptions often do not show on Instagram placements and may only appear on selected surfaces such as Marketplace, Audience Network, Facebook Search Results, or in-stream contexts. Concentrate the core message in the primary text, headline, and visual.

### 1-3. Copy Testing

Prepare at least **three copy variants** per creative concept when budget allows.

| Length | Role | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Very short** | One or two lines that stop the scroll | Known products, simple offers |
| **Medium** | Problem -> solution explanation | Mid-price offers, known categories |
| **Long** | Story or detailed explanation | High-ticket, unknown categories, B2B |
| **Long-form editorial** | Education-led persuasion | Complex services and educational offers |

### 1-4. Hook Design

The hook is the first line of copy or the first 3 seconds of video.

#### Hook Types

| # | Type | Description | Example direction |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Question** | Prompts the user to think | "Still struggling with...?" |
| 2 | **Number / statistic** | Uses data for credibility | "92% of users noticed..." |
| 3 | **Benefit** | States the result directly | "Change X in 3 days" |
| 4 | **Pain point** | Names a frustration | "Tired of...?" |
| 5 | **Story** | Opens a narrative | "Last year I was..." |
| 6 | **FOMO** | Adds urgency or scarcity | "Only 24 hours left" |
| 7 | **Contrarian** | Challenges a belief | "X is not the answer" |
| 8 | **Before/after** | Shows transformation | Before/after visual |
| 9 | **Curiosity gap** | Creates an information gap | "Three mistakes pros avoid" |
| 10 | **Surprise** | Uses an unexpected result | Unusual use case |
| 11 | **Social proof** | Uses third-party validation | Ratings, reviews, user count |
| 12 | **Direct callout** | Names the target audience | "For operations teams..." |
| 13 | **Cliffhanger** | Delays the result | "Wait for the result..." |
| 14 | **Provocation** | Makes a debate-worthy claim | "X is outdated" |
| 15 | **Interaction** | Prompts a vote or quiz | "A or B?" |
| 16 | **Visual satisfaction** | Uses mesmerizing footage | Unboxing, cooking, making process |

#### Hook Rate Planning

Use 3-second views or early retention as a directional signal. Benchmarks vary widely by format, audience, and placement.

### 1-5. CTA Design

#### CTA Button Selection

| CTA button | Best objective | Best business type |
|---|---|---|
| **Shop Now** | Purchase | E-commerce / D2C |
| **Learn More** | Education and consideration | Broadly applicable |
| **Sign Up** | Lead capture or account creation | SaaS, newsletter, webinar |
| **Book Now** | Appointment | Salon, clinic, hotel, service business |
| **Contact Us** | Inquiry | B2B and services |
| **Download** | App or content download | Apps, whitepapers |
| **Apply Now** | Application | Jobs, finance, education |
| **Get Quote** | Quote request | Insurance, remodeling, B2B |
| **Buy Tickets** | Ticket purchase | Events and entertainment |

Rules:

- Match the CTA button to the promise in the copy.
- Send users to a page that matches the CTA expectation.
- Use a specific CTA when the next action is clear.

### 1-6. Ad Angle Design

| Angle | Content | Copy direction |
|---|---|---|
| **Pain point** | Empathy for the problem | "Spending too long on..." |
| **Benefit / USP** | Direct value | "Get X in Y minutes" |
| **Social proof** | Reviews, results, volume | "Trusted by..." |
| **Comparison** | Difference from alternatives | "3x longer than..." |
| **Scarcity / urgency** | Reason to act now | "Ends tonight" |
| **Curiosity** | Open loop | "Why experts avoid..." |
| **Luxury / premium** | Quality and status | "Chef-approved..." |
| **Speed / ease** | Convenience | "Delivered tomorrow" |

## 2. Image Ads

### 2-1. Specs

| Item | Spec |
|---|---|
| Recommended size | Feed: 1440x1800 (4:5), 1440x1440 (1:1); Stories/Reels: 1440x2560 or 1080x1920 (9:16) |
| Minimum size | Feed: 600x750 (4:5), 600x600 (1:1) |
| File type | JPG, PNG |
| File size | Under 30 MB; smaller files load faster |
| Text in image | Keep minimal; the old 20% text rule is no longer a hard rule, but cleaner images often deliver better |

### 2-2. Design Practices

Layout principles:

- **One ad = one message.** Do not cram multiple claims into one image.
- **Prefer 4:5 for Feed** when possible.
- **Use 9:16 for Stories/Reels, not as a Feed image default.**
- **Use high contrast** to stop the scroll.
- **Avoid generic stock imagery** when original product, customer, or brand photography is available.

Effective design patterns:

| Pattern | Description | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Product hero** | Large product image plus benefit text | E-commerce, D2C |
| **Text overlay** | Bold claim over image | Offers, sales, announcements |
| **Before/after** | Split-screen transformation | Beauty, fitness, home improvement |
| **Testimonial** | Customer face plus quote | Trust and social proof |
| **Infographic** | Data or process visualization | Education, B2B |
| **Lifestyle** | Product in use | Brand awareness, apparel, consumer goods |
| **PNG overlay** | Cutout product on background plus caption | Static creative that feels motion-ready |

Brand elements:

- Use logos subtly.
- Keep design system consistent: fonts, colors, layout, image style.

### 2-3. Copy

Primary text:

```
Hook: First line earns attention.
Body: Explain 2-3 benefits or proof points.
CTA: Tell the user what to do next.
```

Headline:

- Keep it short.
- State the benefit or offer directly.

Formatting:

- Use line breaks for readability.
- Use emoji only if it fits the brand and market.
- Use bullets when benefits need scanning.

## 3. Video Ads

### 3-1. Specs

| Item | Spec |
|---|---|
| Recommended size | Reels/Stories: 1440x2560 or 1080x1920 (9:16); Feed: 1440x1800 (4:5) or 1440x1440 (1:1) |
| Minimum size | 120x120; use high-resolution source files for production |
| Recommended length | Feed: 15-60s; Reels: 15-30s as a performance heuristic; Stories: short and direct |
| File type | MP4, MOV; GIF may be accepted for some video placements |
| File size | Up to 4 GB |
| Codec | H.264 video, AAC audio |
| Audio/video technicals | Prefer square pixels, fixed frame rate, progressive scan, stereo AAC 128 kbps+ |
| Frame rate | Up to 30 fps is a practical default unless source and placement justify otherwise |
| Maximum length | Placement-dependent; Instagram Reels ads can be up to 15 minutes, and concise ads usually outperform the theoretical maximum |

### 3-2. Structure Framework

Core structure: Hook -> Body -> CTA

```
[0-3s] Hook          -> Stop the scroll
   ↓
[3-15s] Body         -> Show value: benefit, demo, story, proof
   ↓
[Middle] Soft CTA    -> First action cue
   ↓
[Remaining] Detail   -> More proof, testimonial, FAQ, offer
   ↓
[End] Final CTA      -> Repeat the action
```

#### Safe Zones for 9:16

| Placement | Top margin | Bottom margin | Side margins |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Stories/Reels 9:16 | 14% | 20-35% | 6% each side |

Rule: design with a unified 9:16 safe zone. If the ad may run on Reels, use the conservative 35% bottom margin. Verify every final asset in Ads Manager placement previews because captions, CTAs, and device UI can change the obstruction area.

### 3-3. Design Practices

Audio:

- Add captions by default.
- Make captions large and high contrast.
- Still include music, voiceover, or sound design for users who watch with sound.

Visual:

- Add motion from the first frame.
- Use bold captions and graphic CTAs when appropriate.
- Product cutout overlays can work well when they clarify the offer.
- Choose tone based on the offer: testimonial, interview, demo, documentary, polished, or lo-fi.

Creative types:

| Type | Description | Best use |
|---|---|---|
| **UGC-style / lo-fi** | Natural phone-shot footage | Relatability and authenticity |
| **Testimonial** | Customer explains the experience | Trust, MoF/BoF |
| **Founder / employee** | Face-to-camera message | Brand humanity and trust |
| **Demo / how-to** | Shows product use | Product education |
| **Motion graphics** | Animation or data visualization | B2B, SaaS, complex services |
| **Cinematic** | Brand-film style | Awareness and emotional storytelling |

### 3-4. Copy

Primary text:

- Give a reason to watch or act.
- Add detail the video cannot cover.
- End with a clear CTA.

Headline:

- Condense the core message.
- Make it understandable even if the video is not watched.

## 4. Carousel Ads

### 4-1. Specs

| Item | Spec |
|---|---|
| Cards | 2-10; start with 3-5 |
| Image size | 1080x1080 minimum; use 1440x1440 where available |
| Video size | 1080x1080 minimum; use 1440x1440 where available |
| Video length | Short is usually better; keep each card focused |
| File size | Keep files light for load speed |
| Primary text | Keep concise |
| Card headline | Up to 45 characters before truncation risk |
| Card description | Optional; keep around 18 characters or omit if not needed |
| Aspect ratio | Keep all cards consistent |

### 4-2. Card Structure

Five-beat structure:

```
Card 1: Hook        -> Earn attention and encourage swipe
Card 2: Context     -> State the situation or problem
Card 3: Discovery   -> Introduce the solution or product
Card 4: Reward      -> Proof, testimonial, detail
Card 5: CTA         -> Final action card
```

Effective carousel patterns:

| Pattern | Card structure | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Product catalog** | Product A -> Product B -> Product C | E-commerce and product range |
| **Benefit stack** | Feature 1 -> Feature 2 -> proof -> offer | SaaS and services |
| **Step explanation** | Step 1 -> Step 2 -> Step 3 -> result | How-to and process |
| **Before/after** | Before -> in use -> after -> CTA | Beauty, fitness, home improvement |
| **Storytelling** | Problem -> discovery -> change -> result -> CTA | Brand story |
| **FAQ / objection handling** | Question -> answer -> question -> answer -> CTA | Consideration-stage users |

### 4-3. Design Practices

Card 1 is the gatekeeper:

- Use a strong benefit, curiosity gap, statistic, or transformation teaser.
- Use arrows or visual continuity to show there is more to swipe.

Consistency:

- Keep background, typography, and photo style consistent.
- Use high contrast and minimal mobile-readable text.
- Use visual cues that encourage swiping.

Ordering:

- For storytelling, avoid auto-ordering if it breaks the narrative.
- For product catalogs, automatic ordering can be useful.

### 4-4. Copy

Primary text:

- Summarize the carousel's theme or value.
- Give a reason to swipe.

Card headlines:

- Make each card carry a distinct point.
- Avoid repeating the same message on every card.

### 4-5. Mistakes to Avoid

- Mixing aspect ratios across cards.
- Using heavy files that slow loading.
- Auto-ordering a narrative carousel.
- Reusing Feed designs in Stories without safe-zone adaptation.
- Repeating nearly identical cards.

## 5. Collection Ads

### 5-1. Specs

| Item | Spec |
|---|---|
| Cover | Image or video |
| Product images | Pulled from catalog and displayed in grid-like layouts |
| Headline | Keep short |
| Body text | Keep concise |
| Tap behavior | Opens Instant Experience |
| Requirement | Product catalog connected |

### 5-2. Design Practices

Cover image/video:

- Use the strongest hero visual.
- If using video, communicate brand and product category in the first seconds.
- Lifestyle scenes can work better than isolated product images when context matters.

Product images:

- Use high-resolution, consistent product images.
- Keep product photo style coherent.
- Choose automatic or manual product selection based on merchandising strategy.

### 5-3. Instant Experience Template Selection

| Template | Summary | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Instant Storefront** | Catalog-style product browsing | E-commerce and multi-product sales |
| **Instant Lookbook** | Lifestyle imagery with products | Apparel, interiors, cosmetics |
| **Instant Customer Acquisition** | Lead or action-focused experience | B2B, services, registration |
| **Instant Storytelling** | Immersive brand narrative | Awareness and launches |

### 5-4. Copy

- **Headline:** Category or offer in direct language.
- **Body:** Benefit plus action.
- Because character space is tight, focus on one message.

## 6. Lead Ads / Instant Forms

### 6-1. Specs

| Item | Spec |
|---|---|
| Format | Image or video plus Instant Form |
| Questions | Keep custom questions minimal unless qualification requires more |
| Form type | More volume / Higher intent / Rich creative |
| Autofill | Uses available Facebook profile information where permitted |

### 6-2. Form Design

Form type selection:

| Type | Traits | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **More volume** | Easier completion and higher volume | Newsletter, content download, early lead capture |
| **Higher intent** | Adds review step before submission | Demo request, high-ticket inquiry |
| **Rich creative** | Adds imagery, branding, and product detail | Brand-led lead experience |

Question flow:

```
[Basic information] Name and email with autofill where possible
    ↓
[Segment questions] 1-2 multiple-choice questions to qualify
    ↓
[Thank-you screen] Message plus next-step CTA
```

Lead quality tactics:

- Add custom questions to filter weak-fit leads.
- Use phone or email validation where available and appropriate.
- Connect the CRM early so lead quality can feed follow-up and optimization.

### 6-3. Ad Creative

Image/video:

- Make the offer clear: guide, consultation, quote, demo, webinar, etc.
- Visualize the value: cover image, webinar scene, product/service outcome.
- Image ads are often enough; video is useful but not always required.

Copy:

- **Primary text:** explain what the user gets and why now.
- **Headline:** name the offer directly.
- **CTA:** match the action: Sign Up, Download, Get Quote, Learn More, etc.

### 6-4. Instant Form Intro

Before the questions, explain:

- What happens after submission.
- What benefit the user receives.
- How long it takes.

## 7. Instant Experience

### 7-1. Summary

Instant Experience is a fullscreen mobile experience that opens after an ad tap. It can be used as a Collection Ads destination or as a standalone immersive destination.

### 7-2. Design Practices

Speed:

- It loads faster than a typical mobile web page.
- It can act as a mobile landing-page substitute when the external site is slow or too broad.

Recommended structure:

```
[Header] Brand logo + key message
  ↓
[Hero] Video or image impact
  ↓
[Benefits] 3-4 visual benefit blocks
  ↓
[Social proof] Testimonial or result metric
  ↓
[Product/service] Carousel or grid
  ↓
[CTA] Action button
```

## 8. Creative Testing and Operating Cycle

### 8-1. Testing Principles

- Test at least three copy variants per major creative concept when budget allows.
- Let tests collect enough data before judging.
- Change one meaningful variable at a time when the goal is clean learning.

### 8-2. Weekly Creative Cycle

```
Week 1: Launch 5-10 new creative assets or concepts
  ↓
Week 2: Let them run without unnecessary edits
  ↓
End of Week 2: Judge winners and losers
  - Winners -> Move or adapt into scaling campaign
  - Losers -> Pause or learn from them
  ↓
Week 3: Extract performance insights
  ↓
Week 3: Produce the next creative batch from the insights
  ↓
Repeat
```

### 8-3. Winner Criteria

| Metric | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| CTR | Creative pull and relevance |
| CPA / ROAS | Business efficiency; final decision for conversion campaigns |
| Hook rate / 3-second views | Opening strength |
| Frequency | Fatigue signal |
| Cost per 1,000 people reached | Audience fatigue or auction pressure signal |

### 8-4. Creative Lifespan

- Refresh winning creative after 30-60 days or when fatigue appears.
- Rising frequency plus falling CTR means fatigue is likely.
- Respond to fatigue with creative refresh before bid manipulation.
- Add new creative regularly when scaling.

### 8-5. Scaling Tips

- **Post ID reuse:** When scaling a winning creative into another campaign, reusing the post ID can preserve social proof such as reactions, comments, and shares.
- **Scale by concept:** Scale the winning angle and format logic, not only one ad file.
- **Budget increases:** Avoid abrupt budget jumps; gradual increases reduce learning disruption.
