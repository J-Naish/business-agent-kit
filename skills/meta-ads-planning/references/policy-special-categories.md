# Policy and Special Ad Categories

Use this reference before recommending Meta Ads targeting, structure, creative, or optimization for regulated or constrained categories. Policy is not an afterthought on Meta: it can determine which objectives, targeting controls, audiences, exclusions, copy claims, forms, and creative are available.

## Operating rules

- Check policy category before audience design. A beautiful targeting plan is useless if Special Ad Category rules remove the controls.
- Treat policy and eligibility as account/region/time sensitive. Verify current Ads Manager UI and Meta policy before giving hard instructions.
- Separate **platform policy compliance** from **business/legal compliance**. Meta approval does not prove the advertiser satisfies local law.
- Do not design strategies that depend on restricted or removed controls such as detailed targeting exclusions for sensitive filtering.
- For regulated categories, prefer broad compliant structures, strong creative qualification, clean landing/form disclosures, and first-party/CRM quality loops.
- If a claim affects money, employment, housing, health, safety, identity, or politics, require proof and conservative wording.

---

## 1. Special Ad Category triage

Ask whether the campaign promotes any of the following. If yes, route through the policy checks before media design.

| Category | Examples | Planning impact |
|---|---|---|
| Credit / financial products | Credit cards, loans, mortgages, financing, BNPL, debt relief, insurance-like finance offers | Targeting restrictions, copy/disclosure scrutiny, potential audience limitations |
| Employment | Jobs, recruitment, internships, employment services, professional opportunities | Targeting restrictions, anti-discrimination constraints |
| Housing | Home sales/rentals, mortgages, real estate listings, housing services | Targeting restrictions, anti-discrimination constraints |
| Social issues / elections / politics | Elections, candidates, legislation, social issue advocacy, political fundraising | Authorization, disclaimer, geo/account controls, ad library transparency |

If uncertain, state the ambiguity and ask for the offer, landing page, target country, and claim language.

---

## 2. Planning impact by layer

| Layer | What can change under constraints |
|---|---|
| Objective | Some objectives or conversion locations may be impractical if policy review risk is high |
| Audience | Age, gender, zip/postal, detailed targeting, lookalikes, exclusions, or saved audiences may be limited |
| Custom audiences | Customer lists may be allowed but use, expansion, or exclusions can be constrained by policy and privacy rules |
| Placements | Some surfaces may be unsuitable because of claim visibility, lead quality, or disclosure space |
| Creative | Claims, before/after framing, personal attributes, sensational content, and financial/health promises require conservative wording |
| Forms | Need privacy policy, disclaimers, consent language, qualification questions, and careful handling of sensitive data |
| Measurement | CRM/offline imports must respect consent, data processing, and sensitive data restrictions |
| Reporting | Avoid discriminatory segmentation or optimization interpretations |

---

## 3. Audience design under constraints

### Default compliant posture

- Use geography, language, and broad delivery as the base.
- Use creative, offer, landing page, and form questions to qualify instead of sensitive targeting.
- Use CRM/offline quality feedback where permitted.
- Keep customer exclusions only when policy and current UI allow them.
- Document what is a hard control vs an audience suggestion.

### Avoid

- Inferring or targeting sensitive personal attributes.
- Copy that implies the viewer has a protected characteristic, financial hardship, medical condition, employment status, or personal situation.
- Using removed/unstable controls as the core of the plan, especially detailed targeting exclusions.
- Overly narrow geography/age structures that could create discrimination risk in housing, credit, or employment.
- Lookalike or expansion logic as a substitute for policy review.

## 4. Credit and financial products

Use this for credit cards, loans, mortgages, financing, debt services, and similar offers.

### Planning stance

- Prioritize compliant lead quality over cheap lead volume.
- Use Leads or Sales depending on whether the conversion is an application, quote, consultation, or purchase.
- Prefer qualified lead stages and backend approval outcomes over raw form submissions.
- Use clear disclosures on eligibility, rates, fees, and limitations where required.
- Reconcile platform leads with approval rate, funded accounts, default risk, and contribution margin.

### Creative and copy cautions

| Risk | Safer approach |
|---|---|
| "Are you in debt?" or similar personal-attribute callout | "Debt consolidation options for eligible applicants" |
| Guaranteed approval | State eligibility/approval conditions truthfully |
| Missing cost/rate details | Link to clear terms and disclosures |
| Targeting based on financial hardship | Use broad compliant targeting and form qualification |

---

## 5. Employment

Use this for hiring, recruitment, job boards, internships, and professional opportunity campaigns.

### Planning stance

- Optimize for completed application or qualified applicant when volume allows.
- Use lead forms only when the hiring team can follow up quickly and the form does not collect unnecessary sensitive data.
- Avoid demographic targeting or copy that implies preference for protected classes.
- Measure quality by qualified applicants, interviews, hires, and retention, not raw CPL.

### Creative and copy cautions

| Risk | Safer approach |
|---|---|
| Age/gender-coded language | Role, benefits, requirements, schedule, location |
| "Moms wanted" / "young professionals" | Neutral role and qualification framing |
| Unclear compensation or work terms | Add pay range, location, remote/on-site, requirements where possible |

---

## 6. Housing and real estate

Use this for housing rentals/sales, real estate agents, mortgage-adjacent housing offers, and housing services.

### Planning stance

- Check whether both housing and credit categories apply.
- Use compliant geography and broad delivery; do not use targeting that could exclude protected groups.
- Use creative and landing pages to qualify by property type, price range, location, and availability without discriminatory framing.
- Measure by qualified inquiries, showings, applications, signed leases, or closed transactions.

### Creative and copy cautions

| Risk | Safer approach |
|---|---|
| "Perfect for young families" | "3-bedroom homes near transit and parks" |
| Neighborhood targeting that implies protected-class selection | Property/location facts and broad compliant reach |
| Misleading availability or price | Keep feed/listing availability current |

---

## 7. Social issues, elections, and politics

Use this when the campaign touches elections, candidates, ballot measures, legislation, political organizations, or social issue advocacy.

### Planning stance

- Confirm advertiser authorization, country eligibility, disclaimers, and page/account requirements before launch planning.
- Expect review delays and transparency/ad library implications.
- Use conservative claims and source-backed statements.
- Avoid last-minute launch plans that cannot absorb policy review or authorization delays.

### Measurement stance

- Treat engagement and reach as directional, not persuasion proof.
- Use lift, surveys, site actions, donations, volunteer signups, or list growth where appropriate.
- Be careful with sensitive segmentation and reporting interpretation.

---

## 8. Health, wellness, personal attributes, and sensitive claims

Health and wellness campaigns may not always be Special Ad Category campaigns, but they carry high policy risk.

### Planning stance

- Avoid copy that implies the viewer has a medical condition, body attribute, addiction, mental health state, or sensitive personal status.
- Use educational, service, or benefit framing rather than personal-attribute callouts.
- Require substantiation for efficacy claims, before/after imagery, testimonials, and results claims.
- Use landing pages with appropriate disclaimers and professional credentials when relevant.

| Risk | Safer approach |
|---|---|
| "Struggling with anxiety?" | "Mental wellness support options" |
| "Lose 20 lbs fast" | "Personalized fitness coaching" with realistic claims |
| Before/after body imagery | Product/service demonstration without shaming or unrealistic results |

---

## 9. Teen and age-sensitive targeting

Meta's teen targeting and data-use rules change over time and can vary by region.

Planning stance:

- Verify current age, interest, placement, and data-use limits before recommending teen campaigns.
- Avoid sensitive topics, body image pressure, financial promises, or manipulative urgency.
- Use broad, age-appropriate creative and landing experiences.
- Do not rely on granular targeting or retargeting assumptions without current UI verification.

---

## 10. Lead forms and privacy

For any campaign collecting personal data:

- Include a valid privacy policy URL.
- Add custom disclaimers where the offer requires consent, financial/health disclosure, or follow-up permission.
- Collect only fields needed for the business process.
- Avoid collecting sensitive data inside Meta forms unless there is a clear legal basis and platform permission.
- Define how leads move to CRM, who can access them, and how quickly follow-up happens.
- For Conversion Leads or offline events, confirm consent and data handling for server/CRM uploads.

---

## 11. Review-risk checklist

Before launch, verify:

| Check | Why |
|---|---|
| Category selected correctly | Wrong category can cause rejection, delivery limits, or compliance risk |
| Landing page matches ad claim | Review often checks destination, not just ad copy |
| Disclosures visible | Financial, health, employment, housing, and lead-gen claims need clarity |
| No personal-attribute callouts | Common cause of Meta rejection |
| No misleading urgency/scarcity | Especially risky for finance, health, housing, and politics |
| Audience controls are current | Meta frequently changes available targeting controls |
| CRM/offline uploads are consented | Measurement design can create privacy/compliance risk |
| Creative variants all compliant | Dynamic/flexible/Advantage+ variations still need review |

---

## 12. Output guidance

When policy matters, include a short policy section in the plan:

1. Category assumption and confidence.
2. Target countries/regions.
3. Targeting constraints.
4. Copy/creative constraints.
5. Form/landing disclosure requirements.
6. Measurement/data handling constraints.
7. Items to verify in current Ads Manager or with legal/compliance owner.

Do not present policy-sensitive media recommendations as final until the category and target geography are clear.
